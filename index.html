Course Logo by Rodrigo Rosa

For this skill, i first started by making drafts of what my icon would look like. I wanted to relate both sound, vision and games(since this is a “gamefied” course).



Firstly, i thought of doing something with a dnd("dungeons and dragons") dice and then add some symbols on its sides that represented both sound and image/video, but after messing around with it, i came to the conclusion that maybe a dice with 20 sides would be a bit too complex for an icon, so i gave up that idea. Second try i made a pair of headphones with a cd/dvd in between them, and then added a sort of soundwave to the disc to make it look “cooler”. This was however failing to represent both image and the “gamefication” of the course. In the next tries i played around with poligonal shapes(since people normally associate them with videogames, as well as it makes the icon look simpler) and symbols that could represent both image and sound. In my final try, i made a sort of fusion between a speaker icon and an eye(a sideways eye), and that was the design i decided to go with, as it represented both sound, image, and the angular sides made it look a bit “videogamey”. I then opened inkscape and made my first design. The first design looked nice, however i thought the part representing the iris of the eye looked a bit too small when i zoomed out...


so in the final design i decided to make it a bit bigger. As it was still a bit hard to tell if the icon was also a speaker, i also added some sound waves coming out of it. After i was happy with the design, i then started playing with color pallets until i found the one i liked the most.

Movie Poster by Francisca Paiva - Little Women

Hello, here's my attempt at the movie poster skill!
About the Movie:
Based on the coming-of-age novel written by American novelist Louisa May Alcott, Little Women chronicles the lives of the March sisters—Jo, Meg, Amy, and Beth-, who reside with their mother in post-Civil War America. The story tells of
how they grow up, find love and find their place in the world.
Creation Process:
I read this book several years ago and when the movie came out I wasn't very hopeful about it, since it is extremely
difficult to do justice to a good book through a limited-time movie. However, it really surprised me: Greta Gerwig
managed to create an independent and contemporary piece while staying faithful to its source material - and the presence
of a fantastic cast certainly helped.
For that reason, I thought this movie would be a good choice to make a poster of it.
Since the movie is based on a 19th century book, I thought it would make perfect sense to use an illustration rather
than a photo as the film's image. However, an illustration of any characters would have to portray real actors. I found
this drawing on Google that met those requirements, depicting one of the most iconic images from the movie, so I decided
to use it:


The picture's quality was not the best so I had to enhance it, and then I removed its background. I wanted to convey the idea that the movie tells the story of each of the sisters, together, but also individually, so I thought a good way to do it would be to add a different colored stripe behind each of them. I then chose a palette that I thought would suit the movie, edited the image on Photoshop according to it and added the title and the tagline to match the colors of the stripes. I also added some shadows to these elements, in order to make them more prominent. Since writing also plays a big role in the film, I thought it would be nice to replace the 'i' in the title with a writing quill. Finally, I added the director's and main actor's names using the font 'Poppins' (the same as the tagline), and the billing block. Since this element had little contrast with the sisters' skirts, I decided to add a frame all the way around the poster. The entire creation process was done in Illustrator. Final Result:


Movie Poster by Paulina Wykowska - Interstellar

1. Finding Image I searched artstation.com with the keyword "interstellar". I was inspired by the beautiful artworks that represented space as a lonely place. I decided to use an artistic image of a black hole, since *spoiler alert*, there is a black hole in the movie that looks just the same, and would communicate clearly that the movie is about space.


Composing & Design


The chosen image has a single strong focal point - the black hole itself - which makes it easy to compose around it. I flipped the image vertically so that the brighter part of the background would be on the top. I envisioned it as light guiding the eye from top to bottom of the poster. The black background would also serve perfectly for placing a lot of text (title, billing block and extras). I positioned the picture to my linking on the canvas with the poster aspect ratio and used Content Aware Fill in Adobe Photoshop to fill in the white areas. Next think I wrote "Interstellar" with the Copperplate Gothic Bold font and positioned it. In order to integrate the title into the poster, I had the idea to make the letter T into a spaceship, with smoke coming from the back of it. I also wanted to integrate the billing block in a suitable way, and thought of the billing block "opening" from the middle, creating a path for the spaceship. I drew a placeholder shape of the billing block, then filled it with placeholder text and font. For extra information, I added the names of the most recognizable actors on the top of the poster, using the Cocogoose UltraLight font. After having the composition in place, the rest was polishing and aligning of the elements, filling the billing block with correct information (where I used the Ignotum font). The subtitle has a dim glow effect and a perspective distortion to it, written in Century Gothic. For the "special effects" of the spaceship I used two simple photoshop brushes and a distortion effect for the engine smoke.

PUBLICIST by Lucas Piper

After deciding to make this design based on the Fjällräven Kånken Bags, mainly on their colorful and customizable aspect, the first step was to choose the images that were going to be used. For that, I used the design studio in the Fjällräven's website to create several bags whose colors, after some possible adjustments, would combine well (shown below): the initial goal was to create a quasi-triadic color scheme.


Once I had these images, the next step was to create a jigsaw puzzle-like bag that seemed to be "under construction", but also had some color harmony. In order to achieve this, some offset among the bags was added, different parts of each bag were masked in Photoshop and different color balancing filters were applied. The goal was to minimize the inconsistencies in the saturation and the brightness of the colors, given that a hue-wise selection was already made. In the final design, the assembling bag look was also improved with the use of some splatter brushes and the Path Blur filter that allowed to produce the converging colors close to the bag.


For the text and background colors, I chose to stick with the brand's white and red identity which not only creates some consistency but also allows for a minimalist design that contrasts with the chaotic nature of the assembling bag, in order to guide the viewer's eye into the main element in the middle. Regarding the text, I used Fjällräven Kånken's font (Arctic Fox) and the Frankfurter font on which Arctic Fox was based. These different font weights and sizes aim to establish some visual hierarchy to emphasize the main selling point here: the idea that this product allows for individuality expression by having a possibly unique set of colors chosen by the client. Finally, the weight on the bottom of the poster was pushed towards the left in order to open up some space for the Fjällräven's logo (shown below) to be in the top right corner and, thus, balancing the final result.

Pixel Art by Lucas Piper

Creation Process:
After selecting the Aseprite software for the development of this pixel art animation, the first challenge was to choose a scene that not only led up to some potentially appealing renderings but also allowed for the implementation of Ollie Johnston e Frank Thomas' Twelve Basic Principles of Animation. For that, I decided to animate an astronaut moving in space. Secondly, I chose to use ENDESGA's EDG32 palette that was already available in Aseprite (shown below), given that it allowed for the complementary color scheme that I was aiming for and simultaneously had a range of values for each hue that made possible some differentiation between the character, the foreground and the background. Nevertheless, I only ended up using 19 colors from these 32.

Regarding the character's animation, I began by rendering the full character to have a sense of its main parts in order to later separate and animate them individually. As shown below, the character was drawn with a 21px height, having into consideration an upper right light source and exaggerating the head proportions. Note also that, despite already planning ahead a left-to-right movement, the character was not drawn in profile, since it would detriment its appeal and contradict the Staging principle.


As displayed, the following step was to start by animating the character's bounding box to have a sense of its varying width and height, while applying the Squash and Stretch principle. Next, I animated the astronaut's 3 main parts (the head, the body and the jetpack), adjusting some frames relative to the box animation to preserve the character's area when needed. Once that was done, it was only a matter of rendering the character in each frame, adding some particles and the blinking lights in the bodysuit (as shown in the first and second still frames below). There were also some details that were taken into consideration: 1.The motion of the astronaut, while flying was described following a sinusoidal curve, respecting the Arcs principle;
2.To create the illusion of the jetpack's weight and to add some Follow Through and Overlapping Action, the jetpack ends up describing an overshooting motion in the top point of the jump, as seen in the third frame below;
3.The jump begins with no ease-in but ends with an ease-out motion to create the idea of gravity on the moon and exaggerate the power of the jump. However, the falling motion is almost linear, conveying the notion of low gravity;
4.Both ease-in and ease-out were applied to the horizontal motion.




Regarding the foreground, the lines were drawn using Aseprite's pixel-perfect option to avoid doubles (the same option was used for the outline of the character); jaggies were also avoided. As for the astronaut's shade and the shading in the dunes and the craters, everything was done manually with no dithering tool. In the background, however, the dithering tool was used with the Bayer Matrix 8x8 setting in the sky and Bayer Matrix 4x4 setting in the planet. Besides the planet and the shining stars, a shooting star describing an arc was also included. The final result is 120x120 px.

Audio Visualizer by Miguel Silva

Hello!

When I first saw this skill on the skill tree I immediately planned my path to try this one out! However, this was way harder than I thought, having to use processing and finding documentation of the Minim and FFT library and/or examples was a challenge. Eventually, I found a GitHub repo with an example of how to analyze the track using processing! Now onto the idea, I initially wanted to reproduce an effect similar to when we put a liquid over a speaker, so I drew some cubes evenly distributed in a circle like shape with a sphere in the middle which I quite liked because it was also similar to having a sun effect spreading through the cubes to the edges, so I decided to go with that. So all was left was the hardest part, combining the shape with the actual track analysis. To do so, I divided the whole shape into rows. The rows closest to the center react more to low frequencies and the further we go the higher the frequencies the cubes react. The reaction of a cube to a specific band is determined by the intensity of that band in the current moment, for example, if a song has more low frequencies the closest rows to the center will have their colors significantly more intense than the cubes in the outer rows. The circle in the center represents the lowest of the lowest so it reacts to the "kicks" in the songs. To make this effect more dynamic the stroke and colors of a cube are directly connected to the intensity so If a band has no intensity at all the cubes are "hidden". The cubes also have a rotation that is faster if the music speeds up, the colors vary throughout the music depending on the low, mid, and high levels of the track. Finally, I added a little "pulse" when certain bands are hit distinguishing the cubes that react to them from the rest! The background also has a slight reaction to the current song level. I've tried the visualizer with some different songs but most of them couldn't hit those higher bands and the change of colors can be a bit abrupt sometimes or too bright, however, I feel that I achieved an effect that I enjoy and have multiple interactions with a song, so here you go! (To play any song you like put it in the same folder as the pde file and change the name to "song" or change the "audioFileName" string at the top of the code to the name of the audio file you want to play!)

Like some of my colleagues I started off where I left off with Publicist. I decided to keep it simple as I had done for that submission. For the original image I had underlined smart similarly as the logo to draw attention to both words as I wanted them to have a connection. Here I animated the underline in both the word and the logo to further draw in that comparison.

I made the box and pills move in but from the inside of the frame as give the impression we are peering into something. Then of course everything fades so that the gif may loop.

I used premiere and ezgif to make this submission.


Morphing by Paulina Wykowska

Hi! Here's my pink flamingo snake on the beach. I used Adobe Photoshop for this skill. I used these two pictures:


As for my process: 1.Cut out the snake carefully using the Magnetic Selection tool and some lasso selection corrections 2.Erase the flamingo head using Healing Brush Tool 3.Position the snake and adjust perspective and size 4.Adjust the color of the snake so it's the same as the pink flamingo 5.Add shadows and the bright yellow highlights with a brush. On the head of the snake, I painted the highlights carefully, to preserve the texture of the snake
